Career Development Framework

How Social Cognitive Career Theory guides everything on this site

Why Theory Matters for Your Journey

Career counseling is not guesswork — it is grounded in research about how people develop interests, build confidence, and make decisions. For first-generation students, understanding this process can be especially empowering. You may have been told to "just pick something" without understanding how to explore or why you feel uncertain.

Pathways Forward is built on Social Cognitive Career Theory (SCCT), developed by Lent, Brown, and Hackett (1994, 2000). SCCT explains that career choices are shaped by three interacting forces: what you believe about yourself, what you expect will happen if you pursue a path, and the supports or barriers in your environment. This is not abstract — it directly explains why a student with talent in science might avoid engineering if no one in their family has attended college, or why a strong mentor can change everything.

Self-Efficacy: "Can I Do This?"

Self-efficacy is your belief in your ability to succeed in a specific area. It grows through four experiences: trying something and succeeding, watching others like you succeed, receiving encouragement, and managing anxiety (Bandura, 1997). For first-generation students, self-efficacy is often lower not because of lack of ability, but because of fewer opportunities to practice and observe success in unfamiliar settings (Lent et al., 2000).

Outcome Expectations: "What Will Happen?"

These are your beliefs about the results of a career choice — salary, work-life balance, respect, stability. Students from underserved backgrounds may have limited or inaccurate information about what certain careers actually offer. Our Exploration Tools section helps correct misconceptions and broaden your picture of what's possible.

Environmental Supports & Barriers

SCCT recognizes that career development does not happen in a vacuum. Family encouragement, school counselors, financial aid, and mentorship are supports. Financial strain, lack of role models, discrimination, and limited college knowledge are barriers. Our Equity & Access section directly addresses these factors so they do not silently limit your options.

How SCCT Informs This Site's Activities

Every section of Pathways Forward is intentionally connected to SCCT principles:

Assessment → Self-Efficacy

Interest Assessments Build Awareness

The O*NET Interest Profiler helps you identify areas where you already have curiosity and engagement — a foundation for building self-efficacy. When you discover interests you didn't know you had, you begin to see new possibilities (Lent et al., 1994).

Exploration → Outcome Expectations

Pathway Resources Correct Misinformation

Our curated career and education resources in the Explore section provide realistic information about wages, training requirements, and day-to-day work — helping you form accurate outcome expectations instead of relying on stereotypes or media portrayals.

Action Plan → Goals & Choices

Checklists Translate Insight into Behavior

SCCT emphasizes that interests and goals must lead to action. The interactive checklist and developmental timelines break big decisions into manageable steps, increasing the likelihood that you will follow through (Lent et al., 2000).

Equity → Environmental Factors

Barrier Reduction Is Part of Career Counseling

Professional career counselors do not simply tell students to "work harder." We identify and address systemic barriers. The Equity section connects you to financial aid, mentorship, and support programs that function as environmental supports in the SCCT model.

Our Professional Approach

What This Means for You

As your school counseling team, we use SCCT to guide our work with you. We will help you build confidence through small successes, provide accurate information about careers and education, connect you with mentors and programs, and address barriers — not blame you for them. Career development is a process of learning, trying, reflecting, and adjusting — not a single test result or one perfect answer.

Complementary Perspective: Super's Life-Span Approach

We also draw on Donald Super's (1990) life-span, life-space theory, which reminds us that at your age (16–18), you are in the exploration stage — it is completely normal and developmentally appropriate to be uncertain. Super argued that career maturity means having a plan you can adjust, not having everything figured out. This site supports that exploratory mindset.
