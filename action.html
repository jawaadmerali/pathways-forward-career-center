Action-Oriented Guidance

What do I do next? Practical steps to move from exploration to action

Exploration without action leads to anxiety, not clarity. This section provides concrete next steps aligned with where you are in high school. Use the timelines, resources, and interactive checklist below to build momentum.

Developmental Career Timeline

Based on Super's (1990) exploration stage and standard school counseling practice, here is what to focus on during your junior and senior years:

Junior Year — Fall (September–November)

Take the O*NET Interest Profiler. Meet with your school counselor to discuss results. Begin researching 3–5 career clusters that interest you using the Exploration Tools. Attend college and career fairs.

Junior Year — Spring (January–May)

Schedule job shadowing or informational interviews in fields of interest. Take the PSAT/SAT or ACT. Research post-secondary options (college, trade school, military, workforce). Begin building your resume. Explore summer internship or volunteer opportunities.

Senior Year — Fall (September–November)

Finalize your post-secondary plan. Complete college applications or workforce/training program applications. Submit the FAFSA (opens October 1). Apply for scholarships. Request letters of recommendation. Retake standardized tests if needed.

Senior Year — Spring (January–May)

Compare financial aid offers. Make your decision by May 1 (National College Decision Day). Complete enrollment steps (deposits, housing, orientation registration). For workforce paths, finalize training program enrollment. Celebrate your progress — you have earned it.

Academic & Training Guidance

Course Selection Matters

Choose courses that align with your exploratory interests while meeting graduation requirements. If you are considering healthcare, take biology and chemistry. Interested in technology? Enroll in computer science or coding electives. Dual enrollment at a community college can earn you college credit for free or low cost while still in high school.

Standardized Testing Strategy

Most four-year colleges accept SAT or ACT scores. Many schools are now test-optional, meaning you are not required to submit scores. Research the policies of schools you are interested in. Fee waivers are available for low-income students — ask your counselor. Do not let test costs be a barrier.

Alternative Credentials

If you are pursuing workforce or trade pathways, industry certifications (IT, healthcare, welding, etc.) can be earned while in high school through CTE programs. Ask your counselor about Career and Technical Education offerings at your school or regional center.

Financial Literacy Basics

Financial concerns are one of the biggest barriers for first-generation students (Engle & Tinto, 2008). Understanding these basics reduces anxiety and prevents costly mistakes:

  • FAFSA (Free Application for Federal Student Aid): Required for federal grants, loans, and work-study. Opens October 1 of your senior year. It is free — never pay a company to file it. Visit StudentAid.gov.
  • Grants vs. Loans: Grants (like the Pell Grant) do not need to be repaid. Loans do. Always accept grants and scholarships first. Borrow only what you need.
  • Net Price, Not Sticker Price: The published cost of a college is rarely what you actually pay. Use the College Scorecard and each school's net price calculator to estimate your real cost after financial aid.
  • Scholarships: Apply broadly and early. Use BigFuture Scholarship Search and check with your counselor for local scholarships — local awards often have less competition.
  • Work-Study: A federal program that provides part-time jobs on campus. Earnings do not count against your financial aid in the following year. A good way to gain experience while covering expenses.

Internships, Volunteering & Experiential Learning

Why Experience Matters

SCCT research shows that personal experiences are the strongest source of self-efficacy (Lent et al., 2000). Reading about a career is not the same as trying it. Even short experiences — a day of job shadowing, a week of volunteering — give you real data to inform your decisions.

Where to Find Opportunities

  • Ask your school counselor about local internship programs
  • Volunteer at hospitals, nonprofits, or community organizations related to your interests
  • Search VolunteerMatch.org for local opportunities
  • Apply for summer programs at local colleges (many are free for low-income students)
  • Part-time jobs count — they build skills and professional habits

Networking & Graduate School Preparation

Building Your Network (Yes, as a High School Student)

Networking sounds intimidating, but it simply means building relationships with people who can share knowledge and opportunities. Start here:

  • Informational interviews: Ask adults in careers that interest you for a 20-minute conversation. Most professionals are happy to talk with motivated students. Your counselor can help you identify contacts.
  • Alumni networks: Graduates of your high school who attended colleges or entered careers you are considering are valuable resources. Ask your counseling office about alumni mentorship programs.
  • Professional associations: Many industries have student memberships (e.g., Future Business Leaders of America, HOSA for health careers, SkillsUSA for trades). Joining shows initiative and connects you to competitions and mentors.
  • Thinking ahead to graduate school: If you are considering careers that require advanced degrees (law, medicine, social work, research), know that your undergraduate major matters less than your GPA, experience, and preparation. Focus on building a strong academic record and relevant experience now.
