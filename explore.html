Career & Pathway Exploration

Curated, high-quality resources selected for first-generation high school students

Each resource below has been intentionally selected to help you explore careers, education, and workforce options. Resources are grouped by category with explanations of why they matter for students like you. Use your assessment results as a starting point, then explore broadly.

Career Clusters & Industries

Career Clusters

CareerOneStop — Explore Careers

CareerOneStop, sponsored by the U.S. Department of Labor, organizes hundreds of occupations into 16 career clusters — from Health Science to Information Technology to Manufacturing. Each cluster page shows related careers, required skills, and typical education levels. For first-generation students, this tool replaces the informal career knowledge many peers receive at home, providing a comprehensive map of what's out there.

Industry Research

Bureau of Labor Statistics — Occupational Outlook Handbook

The OOH is the gold standard for career research. It provides detailed profiles of hundreds of occupations including median pay, job outlook, work environment, and education requirements. Use it to build accurate outcome expectations (a key SCCT concept) rather than relying on assumptions about which careers pay well or are in demand.

Local Labor Market

American Job Centers

American Job Centers provide free career counseling, job search assistance, and training program referrals in communities nationwide. For students who plan to enter the workforce directly or pursue certifications, these centers connect you to local employers and apprenticeship programs you might not discover on your own.

College & Graduate Education Pathways

College Search

College Scorecard (U.S. Department of Education)

The College Scorecard lets you compare colleges by cost, graduation rates, earnings after graduation, and student body demographics. For first-generation students, it is especially valuable because it shows whether a school supports students like you — look for schools with high graduation rates for Pell Grant recipients and strong post-graduation earnings relative to cost.

Community College

American Association of Community Colleges — Student Resources

Community colleges offer associate degrees, certificates, and transfer pathways to four-year universities at a fraction of the cost. For financially limited students, starting at a community college is a smart, respected strategy — not a "lesser" option. Many community colleges also offer dual enrollment while you are still in high school.

College Planning

BigFuture by College Board

BigFuture provides step-by-step college planning tools including college search, major exploration, scholarship finders, and application timelines. It is designed for students who are the first in their families to navigate the college application process, with plain-language guides on topics like financial aid, essays, and campus visits.

Workforce Entry, Certifications & Apprenticeships

Apprenticeships

Apprenticeship.gov — Apprenticeship Finder

Registered apprenticeships let you earn a paycheck while learning a skilled trade in fields like healthcare, IT, construction, and advanced manufacturing. Programs typically last 1–4 years and lead to industry-recognized credentials. This is an excellent pathway for students who prefer hands-on learning and want to avoid student debt while building a career.

Trade & Technical Schools

CareerOneStop — Certification Finder

Professional certifications can open doors in fields like IT (CompTIA), healthcare (CNA, phlebotomy), and business without requiring a four-year degree. This tool helps you identify certifications aligned with your career interests and shows which occupations value them. Certifications are often achievable in months, not years.

Skills Building

LinkedIn Learning (Free via Many Libraries)

Many public libraries offer free access to LinkedIn Learning, which provides video courses in software, business, creative skills, and more. For students building professional readiness, completing even one course demonstrates initiative and builds self-efficacy. Ask your school librarian if your library provides access.

Military Pathways

Military Careers

Today's Military

Today's Military, an official Department of Defense resource, provides unbiased information about service branches, career fields, training, benefits, and the GI Bill for education. Military service can provide technical training, leadership experience, and funding for college. This resource helps you explore whether service aligns with your goals — without pressure from recruiters.

ASVAB

ASVAB Career Exploration Program

The ASVAB is not only a military entrance exam — its Career Exploration Program helps all students identify strengths and match them to civilian and military occupations. Many high schools offer the ASVAB CEP for free. Results can complement your O*NET Interest Profiler results by adding an aptitude dimension to your self-discovery process.

Resume & Professional Readiness

Resume Building

Resume.com — Free Resume Builder

Creating a resume as a high school student — even with limited work experience — builds professional identity and self-efficacy. This free builder includes templates designed for students and prompts you to include volunteer work, extracurricular activities, and skills. Having a resume ready makes it easier to apply for internships, summer jobs, and scholarships.

Interview Skills

CareerOneStop — Interviewing for a Job

Interview skills are learned, not innate. This guide covers preparation, common questions, professional dress, and follow-up etiquette. For first-generation students who may not have family members with professional interview experience to practice with, this resource provides a structured starting point. Pair it with mock interviews offered by your school counseling office.

Professional Networking

LinkedIn — Student Profile Guide

Building a LinkedIn profile as a high school student helps you connect with alumni, explore career paths, and discover internship opportunities. Many first-generation students are unaware that professional networking platforms exist. Start with a simple profile listing your school, activities, and career interests — you can grow it over time.
