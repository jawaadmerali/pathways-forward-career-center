Assessment & Self-Discovery

Three types of career assessments: personality, values, and interests

Remember

Results are a starting point for conversation, not a final answer. Discuss them with your counselor (NCDA, 2015).

1 Personality
2 Values
3 Interests

Personality Assessment

Personality assessments explore how you think, communicate, and approach work. They help you understand your natural preferences, not label you permanently.

Personality · Free

HumanMetrics Jung Typology Test

Free personality test based on Carl Jung's and Isabel Briggs Myers' typology. You receive a four-letter type (such as ENFP or ISTJ), a description of your preferences, and career suggestions matched to your type.

HumanMetrics.com also shows communication and learning styles and links your results to compatible work environments. Takes about 10 minutes. No account required.

Take Personality Test →

Values Assessment

Values assessments reveal what matters most to you in a job: creativity, stability, helping others, independence, and more. A career that fits your values is more likely to feel satisfying.

Values · Free

MyPlan Values Assessment

Free online assessment that ranks work aspects across six value clusters based on the Theory of Work Adjustment and O*NET data. You sort cards to show what you need most from a career.

Results include scores for each value cluster and a list of occupations ranked by how well they match your priorities. Takes about 12 minutes.

Take Values Assessment →

Interest Assessment

Interest assessments use the RIASEC model (Realistic, Investigative, Artistic, Social, Enterprising, Conventional) to match you with fields you may enjoy. Holland's theory (Holland, 1997) is the foundation for both options below.

Interests · Paid

Self-Directed Search (SDS)

The gold-standard career interest inventory developed by Dr. John Holland. The SDS asks about your aspirations, activities, competencies, and abilities, then produces a three-letter Summary Code (such as SIA or RIE) linked to occupations in the O*NET database.

Used by counselors worldwide since 1970. Takes about 20-35 minutes. Cost: $9.95 per online session at selfdirectedsearch.com.

Take SDS ($9.95) →

Interests · Free

Holland Code (RIASEC) Short Test

Free short version of a RIASEC interest assessment from the Open-Source Psychometrics Project. Rate 48 work activities on a five-point scale to discover your Holland Code and dominant interest areas.

A good starting point if you want to explore interests before investing in the full SDS. Takes about 5-10 minutes. No account required.

After You Finish

  1. Take all three types if you can: personality, values, and interests.
  2. Look for patterns across your results, not one perfect answer.
  3. Explore suggested careers with curiosity, not commitment.
  4. Discuss results with your counselor.
  5. Test interests through job shadowing, volunteering, or electives.

Limitations

  • Interests are not the same as abilities.
  • Results reflect how you feel today, not forever.
  • Assessments do not account for financial or systemic barriers. See Equity.
  • Best used with a counselor, not instead of one.
