Assessment & Self-Discovery

Explore your interests — assessments expand possibilities, they don't determine your destiny

Important Reminder

No assessment can tell you what you should do with your life. Career assessments are starting points for conversation and exploration, not final answers. Your results reflect your interests at this moment in time — and those can grow and change (NCDA, 2015).

Featured Assessment: O*NET Interest Profiler

The O*NET Interest Profiler is a free, research-based career interest assessment sponsored by the U.S. Department of Labor. It is based on John Holland's RIASEC theory of vocational personalities and work environments (Holland, 1997) and is widely used by school counselors and workforce professionals.

The profiler asks you to rate how much you would enjoy various work activities. Your results place you into one or more of six interest areas: Realistic, Investigative, Artistic, Social, Enterprising, and Conventional (RIASEC). Each area connects to specific career clusters and occupations you can explore further.

Purpose of This Assessment

Why We Recommend It

For first-generation students who may not have exposure to a wide range of careers through family or community networks, the Interest Profiler provides a structured way to discover fields you might not have considered. It aligns with our SCCT framework by helping you identify areas where you may already have curiosity — a foundation for building career self-efficacy (Lent et al., 2000).

Benefits

  • Free, accessible, and available in English and Spanish
  • Links your interest profile directly to real occupations with wage and training data
  • Provides a common language for discussions with your counselor
  • Helps organize exploration when you feel overwhelmed by too many options
  • Can be retaken as your interests evolve

Limitations

Every assessment has limits, and honest counselors acknowledge them:

  • Interests ≠ abilities. Enjoying an activity does not mean you have — or cannot develop — the skills for it. Interest and aptitude are related but distinct (NCDA, 2015).
  • Cultural bias. Some items may reflect middle-class work experiences that do not resonate with all students. Interpret results in the context of your own life.
  • Snapshots, not predictions. Your results reflect how you feel today. Interests change with new experiences — especially during adolescence (Super, 1990).
  • Does not account for barriers. An assessment cannot tell you whether a career is financially accessible or whether you will face discrimination in a field. Those realities matter and are addressed in our Equity section.
  • Not a substitute for counseling. Results are most useful when discussed with a trained professional who can help you interpret them in context.

How to Interpret Your Results

After completing the profiler, follow these steps:

  1. Review your top two or three interest areas. Read the descriptions carefully. Do they resonate with activities you already enjoy — in school, hobbies, or part-time work?
  2. Explore suggested careers with curiosity, not commitment. Click on occupations that sound interesting. Note the education required, typical wages, and work environment.
  3. Look for patterns, not single answers. If your top interests are Social and Investigative, you might explore healthcare, education, psychology, or social research — very different fields that share common themes.
  4. Discuss with your counselor. Bring your results to your next counseling appointment. Ask: "What experiences could help me test whether I actually enjoy this kind of work?"
  5. Try before you decide. Job shadowing, volunteering, or elective courses are low-risk ways to test your interests against reality.

RIASEC Quick Reference

Realistic (hands-on, tools, outdoors) · Investigative (research, analysis, problem-solving) · Artistic (creative expression, design) · Social (helping, teaching, counseling) · Enterprising (leading, persuading, business) · Conventional (organizing, data, detail-oriented work)

Ethical Considerations

Professional career counselors follow ethical guidelines when using assessments (NCDA, 2015):

  • Informed consent: You should know why you are taking an assessment, what it measures, and how results will be used. No one should require you to share results without your permission.
  • Non-discrimination: Assessment results must never be used to steer students away from careers based on gender, race, socioeconomic status, or disability. All pathways should remain open for exploration.
  • Confidentiality: Your results are your information. Counselors should protect your privacy unless you choose to share with family or teachers.
  • Appropriate use: Assessments should supplement — not replace — counseling conversations, experiential learning, and your own reflection.
  • Cultural sensitivity: Counselors should help you interpret results within your cultural context, not impose one "right" way to understand them (Lent et al., 2000).
